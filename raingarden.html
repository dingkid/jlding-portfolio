Rain Garden Exhibit

In Cornerstone of Engineering, our final project was to create an portable, interactive, and accessible museum exhibit to teach grade-school children about one aspect of sustainability. These exhibits are intended to be game boards, so that they are interesting to younger children while still being educational. The rain garden exhibit game board was designed for users to learn about the importance of water conservation and how it can be done through natural means in the city. This project was a group effort with Kieran Hamilton, Lydia Karno, and Thomas Quirin.

During my first major engineeering project, I employed my digital drawing and new CAD skills during the prototyping stage. I did some woodworking while building the game board and learned basic soldering and circuitry for the sensors.


Rain Gardens

The rain garden idea originated from one group member's idea to merge the two broader subjects our group wanted to research - water conservation and city sustainability.

Rain gardens are planted in depressed areas with specific plant species to ensure proper filtration of rainwater. They are primarily used in cities where dirty runoff is present and natural drainage of rain is scarce. Rain gardens in urban areas also create habitats for smaller wildlife, reduce the need for irrigation systems, and bring aesthetic value to homes.

The main idea of this exhibit is to show users (target audience being grade-school children) that water conservation and filtration can be done by your own means. Users can select game pieces (3 plant type, 3 soil type), and place them in different slots on the game board (garden location). The system returns with a score and feedback depending on the quality of each choice (good, okay, bad). Each game piece contains a unique resistor that completes a circuit as it is placed into one of the slots on the board, so the computer can detect which game piece was placed down and in which slot it was placed.

Contributions

My primary roles in this project consisted of:

  • Digitally sketching a preliminary game board concept on CLIP Studio Paint.
  • Being project manager for the prototyping milestone. I modeled our board prototype on SolidWorks and constructed a cardboard prototype. At the end of the week, I compiled a memo and proposed BOM and presented my team's progress to the class.
  • Helping constuct the game board with some basic woodworking skills (gluing and clamping parts, sawing out and sanding slots).
  • Testing and redesigning the sensors to get them to work. When our initial design wasn't working close to the deadline, I came up with a makeshift solution that resulted in more successful detection.

Project Expo

Students from local elementary schools came to Northeastern campus for an afternoon to check out all of the Cornerstone projects. This was the ultimate test to see how successful our projects were.

Some of our observations:

  • Our game pieces worked most of the time and the exhibit held up for the entire expo.
  • Students gave positive reviews of our exhibit.
  • The game setup was not intuitive. Some explanation was needed before users could play the game.
  • This exhibit would not be able to run unattended.

Changes could be made to the clarity of the game board and more work could be done to further improve the sensors. However, it was rewarding to see all of our hard work come together and know that people enjoyed what we made.

The main feedback I got from my prototype presentation was to scale down the final product as the cardboard mockup was much larger than necessary, and to change the moveable game pieces from cubes to flat square prisms so kids know which side the pieces should be placed down.

Assembled topside of game board.

The original sensor concept was to use copper sheets on the sides of the game piece so that they would "click" into the slots. The design was clean, but we did not have the means to make well-toleranced durable parts. My makeshift solution involved using more flexible materials, such as aluminum foil in place of the copper sheets, and cardstock on the bottom of the slot in place of wood.

The first iteration of this... solution that we realized actually worked.

This solution was much less aesthetically pleasing than the original concept, but worked and got us done in time for the Project Expo.
